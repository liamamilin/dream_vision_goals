2  What Is a Dream?

A dream is the first emotional form of a possible future.

It appears before the plan.
Before the goal.
Before the strategy.
Before the milestone.
Before the self knows whether the future is feasible.

The self may only feel:

I want another life.

I want to become different.

I want to build something.

I want to leave this narrow structure.

I want to enter a wider world.

I want to be seen.

I want to become free.

I want to do something that matters.

At this stage, the future is not yet organized.

It may be unclear.
It may be emotional.
It may be symbolic.
It may be exaggerated.
It may be mixed with fantasy.
It may be mixed with pain.
It may be mixed with envy.
It may still contain a real signal.

This chapter begins from a central claim:

A dream is an emotionally charged image of possible future life before that future has been translated into vision, direction, goals, and strategy.

A dream is not yet action.

But it may be the first sign that the self is no longer fully contained by the present.

2.1 Dream as Possible Future

A dream says:

This is not the only possible life.

It opens a future that is not yet real.

The present may be narrow, repetitive, disappointing, or unfinished. The self may live inside work, family expectation, social role, financial pressure, old identity, habit, or survival structure. Then a dream appears and creates distance from the present.

The self begins to imagine:

Maybe I could study somewhere else.

Maybe I could change fields.

Maybe I could build a body of work.

Maybe I could become a different kind of person.

Maybe I could live with more agency.

Maybe I could create something that lasts.

Maybe I could become visible.

Maybe I could be respected for what I build.

Maybe I could build a life around my own values.

The dream does not prove that the future will happen.

It only proves that the current self can imagine beyond current reality.

That matters.

Because before a person can move toward a new life, the new life must first become thinkable.

2.2 Dream Is Not Yet Vision

A dream is not the same as a vision.

A dream may be vague:

I want freedom.

I want success.

I want a better life.

I want to become someone.

I want to escape this situation.

I want to build something meaningful.

A vision is more structured:

I want a life with portable skills, financial runway, self-directed work, visible contribution, healthier rhythm, and enough freedom to make major choices from owned values.

A dream has emotional force.

A vision has structure.

The dream says:

Something is possible.

The vision says:

This is the shape of the possible future.

The dream is earlier.

It should not be forced too quickly into exact form.

But it should eventually be clarified.

If a dream never becomes vision, it may remain emotional mist.

2.3 Dream Is Not Yet Goal

A dream is also not the same as a goal.

A dream may say:

I want to become a writer.

I want to study abroad.

I want financial freedom.

I want to enter a better field.

I want to become healthy.

I want to build a serious project.

A goal says:

Finish this manuscript.

Reach this language score.

Save this amount.

Submit this application.

Build this portfolio.

Complete this project.

Follow this health routine for this period.

A goal has target, boundary, evidence, and review.

A dream has possibility.

A dream may inspire goals, but it is not yet goal.

If the self confuses dream with goal, it may feel inspired but never move.

If the self destroys the dream too quickly with goal pressure, it may lose the emotional source of direction.

The correct movement is:

dream
→ vision
→ direction
→ goal
→ milestone
→ strategy
→ action
→ evidence

The dream is the beginning of this chain.

Not the end.

2.4 Dream Is Not Yet Plan

A dream does not yet know the route.

It may not answer:

How exactly will I get there?

What resources do I need?

What is the timeline?

What comes first?

What are the risks?

What if I fail?

What if the path changes?

These questions matter.

But if they arrive too early, they may crush the dream before it is understood.

The self may say:

I do not know how,
so maybe I should stop wanting it.

This is too fast.

Not knowing how does not mean the dream is false.

It means the dream has not yet become strategy.

The first response to a dream should not be immediate execution.

It should be interpretation.

The self should ask:

What is this dream trying to show me?

What value does it carry?

What future self does it suggest?

What part of my current life does it challenge?

What would need to become true for this dream to become direction?

Planning comes later.

Understanding comes first.

2.5 Dream Carries Emotional Possibility

A dream is not only an idea.

It is emotionally charged.

The self may feel:

longing

excitement

pain

hope

restlessness

envy

sadness

urgency

fear

aliveness

tension

This emotion is important.

It shows that the dream touches something alive inside the self.

A neutral possibility may not become dream.

For example:

It is possible to learn a random skill.

It is possible to move to a random city.

It is possible to pursue a random career.

But these are not necessarily dreams.

A dream carries inner charge.

It says:

This future matters to me somehow.

The self should ask:

What emotion appears when I imagine this future?

Hope?

Relief?

Pride?

Freedom?

Recognition?

Peace?

Power?

Belonging?

Revenge?

Escape?

Why does this future feel charged?

The emotional charge is evidence.

But it must be interpreted carefully.

2.6 Dream Carries Value

A dream usually contains value.

A person dreams of freedom because agency matters.

Dreams of recognition because witness matters.

Dreams of study because growth matters.

Dreams of wealth because security, dignity, or option space matters.

Dreams of love because connection matters.

Dreams of creation because expression and contribution matter.

Dreams of health because the body matters.

Dreams of another country because environment, identity transition, or possibility expansion matters.

The self should ask:

What value is inside this dream?

What does this dream protect?

What does it promise?

What lack does it answer?

What future self does it support?

For example:

Dream:
I want to study abroad.

Possible values:
expansion, education, legitimacy, mobility, identity transition, access, future possibility.
Dream:
I want to write books.

Possible values:
expression, structure, contribution, visible thought, intellectual identity.
Dream:
I want financial freedom.

Possible values:
security, agency, dignity, self-direction, reduced dependency.
Dream:
I want to be recognized.

Possible values:
accurate witness, contribution, dignity, voice, repair of invisibility.

The dream is a container.

The value inside it must be extracted.

2.7 Dream Carries Need

A dream may also contain need.

The self may dream of what it lacks.

A dream of freedom may contain a need for agency.

A dream of wealth may contain a need for safety.

A dream of love may contain a need for attachment.

A dream of recognition may contain a need to be seen.

A dream of status may contain a need for dignity.

A dream of another place may contain a need for distance from old roles.

The self should ask:

What need is inside this dream?

What does my current life fail to provide?

What hunger does this future answer?

Is this need current?

Is it old?

Is it excessive?

Is it legitimate?

Is the dream the best way to answer this need?

A dream becomes clearer when need is named.

Otherwise the self may chase a symbol without understanding the lack.

2.8 Dream Carries Wound

Some dreams are intensified by wound.

A person who felt invisible may dream of public recognition.

A person who felt powerless may dream of control.

A person who felt poor may dream of wealth.

A person who felt trapped may dream of total freedom.

A person who felt humiliated may dream of status.

A person who felt rejected may dream of being chosen.

This does not make the dream false.

It means the dream carries pain.

The self should ask:

What wound is inside this dream?

What pain does this future promise to repair?

Can this dream actually repair that wound?

What part of the dream is value?

What part is compensation?

What healing is needed beyond achievement?

A wound can point toward a real value.

But if wound governs the dream completely, the dream may become distorted.

The self may not pursue freedom.

It may pursue escape.

It may not pursue recognition.

It may pursue proof.

It may not pursue love.

It may pursue rescue.

The wound must be named so the value can be saved.

2.9 Dream Carries Identity

A dream often contains a future identity.

The self does not only want different conditions.

It wants to become someone.

a writer

a builder

a scholar

a creator

a free person

a respected professional

a healthy person

a loved person

a courageous person

a visible person

a person with options

a person who contributes

The dream shows a possible self.

The self may say:

I am not yet that person,
but I can imagine becoming that person.

This imagination matters.

Identity often changes first in possibility before it changes in evidence.

But imagined identity must eventually become practiced identity.

The self should ask:

Who am I becoming in this dream?

What identity does this dream suggest?

What evidence would make that identity real?

What practice would train that identity?

What old identity must be released?

A dream becomes serious when the future identity can be translated into practice.

2.10 Dream Carries Atmosphere

Sometimes the dream is not a specific achievement.

It is an atmosphere.

The self may dream of a life that feels:

free

quiet

serious

beautiful

respected

creative

stable

open

dignified

adventurous

warm

powerful

peaceful

alive

This matters.

An atmosphere may reveal values that the self cannot yet name.

For example:

A dream of quiet life may reveal peace, health, recovery, and simplicity.

A dream of serious life may reveal craft, discipline, meaningful work, and dignity.

A dream of open life may reveal freedom, mobility, and possibility.

A dream of beautiful life may reveal aesthetic value, vitality, and environment.

A dream of powerful life may reveal agency, competence, and control over conditions.

The self should ask:

What atmosphere does this dream carry?

What values create that atmosphere?

What current life conditions block it?

What practices would create a small version of it now?

Not every dream begins as a clear object.

Sometimes the future first appears as atmosphere.

2.11 Dream Carries Symbol

A dream often attaches itself to symbols.

The self may dream of:

a university

a city

a country

a company

a title

a book

a house

a relationship

a body

a public identity

a certain income

a lifestyle image

These symbols are not meaningless.

They carry emotional value.

But the symbol must be interpreted.

For example:

A university may symbolize legitimacy, access, transition, and a wider world.

A city may symbolize reinvention and freedom.

A title may symbolize competence and recognition.

A book may symbolize completed thought and contribution.

A house may symbolize stability and belonging.

A relationship may symbolize being chosen and shared life.

A body may symbolize vitality, attractiveness, control, or self-respect.

The self should ask:

What does this symbol mean?

Do I want the symbol itself?

Or the value it represents?

Can the value be built through this symbol?

Could it be built another way?

What danger appears if I confuse symbol with value?

A symbol can guide the self.

But it should not replace the value.

2.12 Dream as Signal, Not Command

A dream should be treated as signal.

Not command.

A signal says:

Pay attention.
Something here matters.

A command says:

Obey immediately.
This must become your life.

The self should not obey every dream.

Some dreams are immature.

Some are borrowed.

Some are impossible.

Some are compensatory.

Some are escape.

Some are useful only as clues.

But the self should not dismiss dreams either.

A dismissed dream may contain a neglected value.

The mature response is:

I will listen to this dream.

I will not surrender to it blindly.

I will interpret it.

I will test it.

I will extract its value.

I will decide whether it should become direction.

Dream is evidence.

But it is not final authority.

2.13 Dream and Aliveness

A dream often brings aliveness.

The self may feel more awake when imagining it.

This aliveness can be important.

It may show that the dream connects to values suppressed in current life.

The self may feel:

When I imagine this, I feel energy.

When I think about this future, something in me wakes up.

When I picture this path, the present feels less closed.

When I imagine building this, I feel more like myself.

Aliveness is not proof that the dream is feasible.

But it is evidence that the dream touches something real.

The self should ask:

What exactly feels alive here?

The work?

The identity?

The recognition?

The freedom?

The environment?

The contribution?

The escape?

The status?

The possibility?

Aliveness must be specified.

If it is real value, it should be protected.

If it is only fantasy intensity, it should be clarified.

2.14 Dream and Restlessness

A dream may also appear as restlessness.

The self may not know what it wants, but it knows the current form is not enough.

It feels:

I cannot stay here forever.

This role is too small.

This path does not contain my future.

This life is not false,
but it is incomplete.

Something else must be built.

Restlessness can be useful.

It shows mismatch between present structure and future possibility.

But restlessness can also become chronic dissatisfaction.

The self should ask:

What exactly feels too small?

What value is not being lived?

What future is asking for space?

Is this restlessness calling me toward growth?

Or only refusing commitment to the present?

Restlessness becomes direction only when it is named.

2.15 Dream and Dissatisfaction

Many dreams begin in dissatisfaction.

The self feels the present is not enough.

But dissatisfaction must be interpreted carefully.

It may mean:

A real value is neglected.

A current role has become too small.

A future self is emerging.

A borrowed life is losing authority.

A new direction is needed.

It may also mean:

The self is comparing too much.

The self is avoiding current responsibility.

The self wants emotional escape.

The self cannot tolerate ordinary difficulty.

The self is chasing novelty.

The self should ask:

What is the source of dissatisfaction?

Is it value-based?

Comparison-based?

Shame-based?

Fatigue-based?

Avoidance-based?

What would remain after rest, honesty, and comparison reduction?

A dream born from dissatisfaction must be tested.

Some dissatisfaction is guidance.

Some is distortion.

2.16 Dream and Borrowed Future

A dream may be borrowed.

The self may dream of a future because others admire it.

It may copy a future from:

family expectation

school ranking

market images

online lives

professional prestige

peer comparison

cultural scripts

status hierarchy

The borrowed future may look attractive.

But the self must ask:

Whose future is this?

Did I choose it?

Or did I absorb it?

Do I want the practice behind this future?

Or only the image?

Would I still want it if no one admired it?

Does this future fit my values, body, temperament, and season?

A borrowed dream can still contain useful value.

But it must be converted into owned form.

Otherwise the self may spend years pursuing a future that does not belong to it.

2.17 Dream and Comparison

Comparison can create dreams.

The self sees someone else’s life and begins to imagine a similar future.

This may open possibility.

It may also distort direction.

The self may think:

They have that life.

I want that life.

Therefore that should be my future.

But the self usually sees only a visible fragment.

It sees:

status

recognition

money

relationship

freedom

confidence

beauty

public success

It may not see:

cost

history

support

temperament

hidden pain

daily practice

constraints

private trade-offs

The self should ask:

Do I want their whole life?

Or one value their life symbolizes?

What exactly am I comparing?

What desire did this comparison awaken?

What is my version of that value?

Comparison should become information.

Not borrowed destiny.

2.18 Dream and Feasibility

A dream does not need to be immediately feasible to be worth examining.

But it must eventually face feasibility.

There are stages.

At first, ask:

What does this dream reveal?

Later, ask:

Can this dream become vision?

Then:

Can this vision become direction?

Then:

Can this direction become goals?

Then:

Can these goals become strategy?

Then:

Can this strategy survive reality?

Feasibility should not be ignored.

But feasibility should enter at the right layer.

If the self begins with feasibility too early, it may kill possible futures before understanding them.

If the self never asks feasibility, it may remain in fantasy.

The mature self asks both, in order.

2.19 Dream and Cost

A dream becomes serious when the self asks what it costs.

Many dreams are attractive because their cost is hidden.

The self imagines:

the arrival

the success

the identity

the freedom

the recognition

the new life

But serious dreaming asks:

What must be sacrificed?

What must be learned?

What must be endured?

What must be built?

What must be refused?

What old identity must die?

What risk must be accepted?

What daily practice would this require?

For example:

Dream:
Become a writer.

Cost:
Write regularly, revise, finish, accept feedback, face exposure, tolerate slow recognition.
Dream:
Study abroad.

Cost:
Language preparation, application work, financial planning, uncertainty, cultural transition, possible rejection.
Dream:
Become free.

Cost:
Skill, money, discipline, boundary, risk, responsibility.

A dream that cannot face cost is still immature.

A dream that can face cost may become direction.

2.20 Dream and Responsibility

A dream asks for responsibility if it becomes chosen.

The self cannot only enjoy the image.

It must eventually ask:

What responsibility follows if I choose this dream?

What must I practice?

What must I stop avoiding?

What must I learn?

What resources must I build?

What consequences must I accept?

What people may be affected?

What risk must I manage?

A dream of freedom requires responsibility for structure.

A dream of love requires responsibility for care and repair.

A dream of recognition requires responsibility for real contribution.

A dream of meaningful work requires responsibility for completion.

A dream of health requires responsibility for rhythm.

A dream of change requires responsibility for transition.

Without responsibility, dream becomes fantasy.

Responsibility is the beginning of dream maturity.

2.21 Dream and Evidence

At the dream stage, evidence may be weak.

But the self can still begin looking for early evidence.

Evidence may include:

repeated return to the dream

small actions already taken

skills already emerging

values repeatedly present

energy when practicing related actions

feedback from reality

existing strengths

willingness to sacrifice

ability to create first steps

The self should ask:

What evidence shows this dream is not random?

Have I returned to it over time?

Have I already practiced anything related to it?

What small action could test it?

What evidence would make it more credible?

What evidence would show it is only fantasy?

A dream does not need full proof at the beginning.

But it should begin producing evidence if it is to become direction.

2.22 Dream and Small Tests

A dream can be tested before it becomes a full commitment.

The self can create small experiments.

For example:

If I dream of writing,
write one essay or chapter.

If I dream of studying abroad,
research programs and take a language practice test.

If I dream of changing fields,
complete one project in the new field.

If I dream of public contribution,
publish one bounded artifact.

If I dream of health,
commit to one recovery routine.

If I dream of a new career,
interview someone in that field.

If I dream of freedom,
build one month of runway or reduce one dependency.

Small tests protect the self from both cynicism and fantasy.

They say:

Do not only imagine.

Do not overcommit blindly.

Create evidence.

A dream that survives small tests may deserve more structure.

2.23 Dream and Time

Some dreams are temporary.

Some return for years.

Some belong to a season.

Some belong to a future identity.

A passing dream may be only emotional weather.

A recurring dream may reveal a deeper value.

The self should ask:

How long has this dream been present?

Does it return after mood changes?

Does it appear across different life stages?

Has it survived comparison, fatigue, and delay?

Does it become stronger with practice?

Or only stronger in fantasy?

Time helps distinguish signal from impulse.

A dream that returns repeatedly may deserve attention.

But repetition alone is not enough.

It still needs examination, cost, and evidence.

2.24 Dream and Present Reality

A dream should not be used to despise the present blindly.

The present may be incomplete, but it contains resources.

It contains:

skills

money

relationships

time

body

knowledge

constraints

responsibilities

habits

unfinished tasks

existing evidence

current identity

The dream must eventually meet the present.

The self should ask:

Where am I now?

What resources do I have?

What constraints are real?

What responsibilities must be honored?

What can be used as starting material?

What must be changed?

What must be accepted temporarily?

Dream without present reality becomes fantasy.

Present reality without dream becomes confinement.

The mature self brings them into contact.

2.25 Dream and Future Self

A dream often reveals a possible future self.

The self should ask:

Who is the future self in this dream?

What does this future self know?

What can this future self do?

What values does this future self live by?

What environment supports this future self?

What evidence proves this future self became real?

What present action would serve this future self?

Future self gives the dream identity direction.

For example:

Dream:
Build a serious intellectual life.

Future self:
A person who writes, thinks structurally, completes work, shares contribution, and is recognized for useful clarity.
Dream:
Become free.

Future self:
A person with options, skills, financial runway, boundary, and self-directed decisions.
Dream:
Become healthy.

Future self:
A person who trusts the body, protects rhythm, and can sustain work and love.

The future self makes the dream more concrete.

2.26 Dream and Direction Potential

Not every dream should become a direction.

A dream has direction potential if it contains:

real value

repeated emotional return

possible future self

willingness to face cost

some early evidence

compatibility with core values

potential for practice

room for strategy

capacity for revision

A dream has weak direction potential if it contains mostly:

status image

avoidance

revenge fantasy

comparison panic

unwillingness to practice

refusal of cost

no connection to values

no evidence over time

no responsibility

The self should ask:

Does this dream deserve to become a direction?

Or should it remain a signal only?

Should I explore it?

Test it?

Convert it?

Release it?

This is the first major judgment in dream work.

2.27 The Dream Reading Map

A dream can be read through a map.

Dream:
What future do I imagine?

Emotional Charge:
What do I feel when I imagine it?

Symbol:
What image, object, place, role, or status carries the dream?

Value:
What value is inside it?

Need:
What need does it answer?

Wound:
What pain may intensify it?

Future Self:
Who am I becoming in the dream?

Present Lack:
What current condition does it challenge?

Borrowed Element:
What part may come from comparison, family, culture, market, or status?

Fantasy Element:
What part avoids cost or reality?

Responsibility:
What would I need to carry if I chose it?

Cost:
What would it require?

Evidence:
What already supports it?

Small Test:
What experiment can test it?

Decision:
Ignore, observe, test, structure, or commit?

This map turns dream into examinable material.

2.28 Example: Dream of Freedom

Dream:
I want to become free.

Emotional Charge:
Relief, energy, urgency, fear.

Symbol:
Open time, independent work, money runway, ability to choose.

Value:
Freedom, agency, dignity, self-direction.

Need:
Decision space and reduced dependency.

Wound:
Possible history of feeling trapped or controlled.

Future Self:
A person with skill, options, boundary, and self-directed life.

Present Lack:
Current life feels constrained or externally governed.

Borrowed Element:
Possibly influenced by social images of freedom.

Fantasy Element:
May imagine freedom as absence of all responsibility.

Responsibility:
Build money, skill, discipline, and risk management.

Cost:
Uncertainty, effort, delayed comfort, possible disapproval.

Evidence:
Repeated desire for agency; existing skill-building; planning behavior.

Small Test:
Build one concrete option, one project, or one month of runway.

Decision:
Keep the dream, but define freedom as structured agency.

Freedom becomes serious when it is separated from escape.

2.29 Example: Dream of Recognition

Dream:
I want to be recognized.

Emotional Charge:
Longing, envy, pain, excitement.

Symbol:
Audience, praise, public work, respected identity.

Value:
Recognition, contribution, dignity, voice.

Need:
Accurate witness and social reality.

Wound:
Old invisibility, dismissal, or not being taken seriously.

Future Self:
A person whose work is visible, useful, and respected.

Present Lack:
Work may feel private, unseen, or unfinished.

Borrowed Element:
Possible status comparison or online visibility pressure.

Fantasy Element:
May imagine recognition without long practice or exposure.

Responsibility:
Create real contribution, finish work, share it, accept feedback.

Cost:
Exposure, criticism, patience, delayed reward.

Evidence:
Repeated creation, desire to publish, need for serious feedback.

Small Test:
Share one bounded artifact with an appropriate audience.

Decision:
Convert recognition dream into accurate witness for real contribution.

Recognition becomes healthy when it serves contribution.

2.30 Example: Dream of Study Abroad

Dream:
I want to study abroad.

Emotional Charge:
Hope, fear, expansion, urgency.

Symbol:
University, country, new identity, global environment.

Value:
Growth, mobility, legitimacy, access, future possibility.

Need:
New field, wider world, structured transition, identity renewal.

Wound:
Possible fear of stagnation or being trapped in current role.

Future Self:
A person with stronger credentials, broader exposure, and new direction.

Present Lack:
Current environment may feel too narrow or limiting.

Borrowed Element:
Prestige and social status may influence the dream.

Fantasy Element:
May imagine that a new place automatically solves identity problems.

Responsibility:
Language preparation, applications, finances, recommendation, portfolio, transition planning.

Cost:
Money, time, uncertainty, rejection risk, cultural adaptation.

Evidence:
Prior academic ability, project work, written materials, learning capacity.

Small Test:
Research programs, take language diagnostic, build application evidence.

Decision:
Keep dream if connected to owned direction, not prestige alone.

Study abroad becomes serious when it becomes structured transition.

2.31 Example: Dream of Meaningful Work

Dream:
I want to build meaningful work.

Emotional Charge:
Aliveness, seriousness, pressure, hope.

Symbol:
Book, project, framework, public artifact, intellectual identity.

Value:
Truth, creation, contribution, structure, recognition.

Need:
Expression, visible thought, future identity.

Wound:
Possible pain from unfinished potential or invisibility.

Future Self:
A person who completes, contributes, and is recognized for serious work.

Present Lack:
Work may be scattered, unfinished, or private.

Borrowed Element:
May include status image of being an author or thinker.

Fantasy Element:
May imagine identity without completion.

Responsibility:
Write, revise, finish, share, accept feedback, build body of work.

Cost:
Time, attention, exposure, delayed recognition, discipline.

Evidence:
Repeated writing, frameworks, chapters, strong conceptual interest.

Small Test:
Complete and share one bounded piece of work.

Decision:
Keep dream and translate it into completion cycles.

Meaningful work becomes real through artifacts.

2.32 What Makes a Dream Worth Keeping?

A dream is worth keeping if it continues to reveal real value after examination.

It may be worth keeping if:

It connects to core values.

It returns across time.

It becomes stronger through practice, not only fantasy.

It creates aliveness.

It points to a future self the self respects.

It can accept cost.

It can produce evidence.

It can be tested.

It can be revised.

It does not require self-betrayal.

It can become direction.

A dream may need revision if:

It is mixed with status hunger.

It depends heavily on comparison.

It hides escape.

It avoids responsibility.

It exaggerates what the future will repair.

It ignores cost.

It treats symbol as value.

It is borrowed from others.

A dream may need release if:

It no longer connects to owned values.

It consistently produces false direction.

It requires unacceptable sacrifice.

It belongs to an old self.

It is only revenge against shame.

It cannot survive contact with reality.

It creates emptiness when approached.

Keeping, revising, and releasing dreams are all part of maturity.

2.33 Dream Maturity

A dream matures through several transformations.

emotional possibility
→ symbolic image
→ value interpretation
→ future self
→ cost awareness
→ small test
→ evidence
→ vision
→ direction

At first, the dream is only felt.

Then it is named.

Then it is interpreted.

Then it is tested.

Then it either strengthens, changes, or dissolves.

A mature dream can say:

I know what value I carry.

I know what need I answer.

I know what illusion I contain.

I know what cost I require.

I know what first evidence should be created.

I am ready to become vision.

A dream that cannot mature remains fantasy.

A dream that matures becomes the seed of direction.

2.34 What Is a Dream?

A dream is not a plan.

It is not yet a goal.

It is not yet a strategy.

It is not yet a promise that the future will happen.

A dream is the first emotional appearance of possible future life.

It carries:

possibility

value

need

wound

identity

symbol

atmosphere

future self

aliveness

risk

responsibility

The mature position is:

I will not dismiss a dream because it is not yet practical.

I will not obey a dream because it feels powerful.

I will read the dream.

I will ask what value it contains.

I will ask what need it answers.

I will ask what wound it may carry.

I will distinguish symbol from value.

I will distinguish dream from borrowed image.

I will distinguish possibility from fantasy.

I will ask what cost the dream requires.

I will create small evidence.

I will decide whether the dream should become vision and direction.

A dream is the beginning of future architecture.

It says:

Another future may be possible.

The work of this chapter is to listen carefully to that sentence without becoming its prisoner.

A dream should neither be worshiped nor killed.

It should be understood.

Then tested.

Then transformed.
